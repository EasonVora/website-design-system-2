Web Design Fonts

Using the right fonts in web design is crucial because they significantly impact readability and user experience, ensuring that content is easily digestible. Appropriate font choices also convey brand identity and evoke emotions, influencing how users perceive the website and its message. Additionally, a well-selected font can enhance the overall aesthetics of the site, creating a cohesive and professional look that engages visitors.

Serif

Noto Serif

Link to font code

Noto Serif features a modulated serif design ideal for Latin, Cyrillic, and Greek texts, and it works well as a complementary font to other script-specific Noto Serif styles. This typeface includes a range of italic styles and offers multiple weights and widths. Additionally, it contains 3,256 glyphs to accommodate diverse typographic requirements.

IBM Plex Serif

Link to font code

IBM Plex® Serif is IBM's corporate typeface and an open-source project created by the Brand & Experience team. It reflects the company's identity and the relationship between humans and machines in a refined serif style. With its engineered precision and approachable design, Plex Serif is perfect for editorial storytelling and offers excellent legibility across various platforms. It pairs well with other fonts in the Plex family, adding depth and contrast, while its expressive italic options enhance creative design flexibility.

Roboto Serif

Link to font code

Roboto Serif is a variable typeface family created to ensure a smooth and comfortable reading experience. Its minimalistic and highly functional design makes it suitable for various applications, including app interfaces, thanks to a wide range of weights and widths across multiple optical sizes. Although it was meticulously designed for digital media to perform well at various sizes and resolutions, it is equally effective for reading and working in print.

Sans Serif

Noto Sans

Link to font code

Noto Sans is an unmodulated sans serif typeface designed for Latin, Cyrillic, and Greek texts, making it a great complementary option for other script-specific Noto Sans fonts. It features various italic styles, multiple weights and widths, and includes a total of 3,741 glyphs.

IBM Plex Sans

Link to font code

IBM Plex® Sans is IBM's corporate typeface and an open-source project developed by the Brand & Experience team (BX&D). This versatile sans serif typeface embodies IBM's brand identity and features a neutral yet approachable Grotesque style, ensuring excellent legibility across print, web, and mobile platforms. The Plex family includes other styles like Sans Condensed, Mono, and Serif, all designed to work harmoniously together while supporting a wide range of scripts.

Roboto

Link to font code

Roboto combines a mechanical skeleton and geometric forms with friendly, open curves. Unlike some grotesque typefaces that distort letterforms for a rigid rhythm, Roboto maintains a natural width, providing a smoother reading experience similar to humanist and serif fonts. This origin family can be effectively paired with the Roboto Condensed and Roboto Slab families for added versatility.

Monospace

Noto Sans Mono

Link to font code

Noto Sans Mono is a monospaced sans serif typeface specifically designed for programming and other applications that require a fixed-width font. It supports Latin, Cyrillic, and Greek scripts, along with various symbols. The typeface offers multiple weights and widths, featuring a total of 3,787 glyphs for versatile use.

IBM Plex Mono

Link to font code

IBM Plex® is IBM's corporate typeface and an open-source project developed by the Brand & Experience team (BX&D). The monospaced variant, IBM Plex® Mono, is specifically designed for coding and technical applications, ensuring excellent legibility and clarity in fixed-width environments. While it complements the other styles in the Plex family—Sans and Serif—it excels in delivering a consistent reading experience, supporting a wide range of scripts for versatile use.

Roboto Mono

Link to font code

Roboto Mono is a monospaced typeface in the Roboto family, designed for optimal readability on screens across various devices. While related to its variable-width counterparts, it adapts its forms to suit a monospaced environment, with features like serifs on narrow glyphs for improved texture and adjustments to the weight of wider glyphs. Special attention is given to distinguishing similar-looking characters, making it easier to differentiate between digits and letters, such as '1', 'l', and 'I', as well as punctuation like curly braces and periods, enhancing clarity and efficiency for software source code.

Bad Fonts

Danfo

Link to font code

Danfo, a variable-axis font inspired by vinyl lettering on Lagos buses, should be used sparingly in web development due to its specialized design. While its cultural and artistic appeal is strong, its intricate style may not suit all content and could reduce readability in large amounts. Additionally, its unique aesthetic is best reserved for specific, thematic applications rather than extensive use across a website.

Kablammo

Link to font code

Kablammo, an experimental variable font inspired by '90s designs and cartoons, features playful, decorative elements that shift in weight and contrast. While its dynamic nature and animated potential are appealing, using it excessively in web development can overwhelm users and harm readability. Its intricate design and morphing glyphs may distract from content and slow down site performance, making it better suited for limited, creative uses rather than as a primary font.
